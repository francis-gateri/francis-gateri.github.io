How I Started My Freelance Content Writing Career

A personal story and step-by-step guide on how to break into freelance content writing and beyond. Real platforms, real rates, real lessons from someone who has done it.

FG

Francis Gateri

Professional Writer & Developer

May 2026
Personal Story
8 min read

I first heard about freelance content writing from a friend back on campus. I thought it was just being given a task to write an article by a stranger, agreeing on payment terms, and that was the end of the story.

I did not know that freelance content writing is an actual profession, one that combines creativity, marketing, and storytelling to help businesses grow.

Freelance writing is a growing digital career in Kenya. Platforms like Fiverr, Upwork, and LinkedIn offer real chances of building a career as a freelance content writer. If you are wondering how to start your own journey, this guide is both my story and a step-by-step guide to help you begin, with real lessons I learned along the way.

Freelance content writing is not just a side hustle. It is a profession that combines creativity, marketing, and storytelling to help businesses grow.

1

Discover What Content Writing Really Means

When I started, I thought content writing was just about good, polished English. But as I progressed in my career, I realized that clients were not primarily interested in your grammar. They were interested in how you could help them achieve their purpose and goals.

For example, clients want writing that helps them:

  • Generate leads in their businesses
  • Build authority in their area of expertise
  • Educate their readers and grow trust
  • Improve their SEO rankings on Google

In short, what you write must help achieve something measurable. It is not creative writing for its own sake. Every piece connects to a business goal.

Recommended Reading

Read brand blogs like HubSpot and Buffer and notice how every article connects to a marketing goal. They will help you understand that every article you write must be part of a bigger strategy.

2

Learn the Skills That Matter and the Tools That Help

I did not start with paid courses. Everything I learned came from free platforms like HubSpot Academy, Google Digital Garage, and YouTube tutorials.

Here is what you actually need to master as a content writer:

  • Research and SEO basics so your writing ranks on Google and reaches the right audience
  • Storytelling and tone so readers do not just skim your words but actually feel them
  • Editing and structural clarity because a well-structured article is what separates amateur writing from professional work

Free Resources That Actually Work

HubSpot Academy — For SEO, blogging, and content marketing fundamentals. Free certification included.

Grammarly — For editing, tone checks, and catching errors before your client does.

Google Trends — For finding what topics people are actively searching for right now.

Google Digital Garage — For understanding digital marketing and how content fits into it.

Through HubSpot Academy specifically, I learned about content marketing, its relationship to content writing, and SEO optimization, all for free. That knowledge directly helped me land better clients.

3

Build Your Portfolio Before You Apply Anywhere

This step changed everything for me.

Before expecting clients to pay you, you need to show what you can do. That is where a portfolio becomes critical. A portfolio speaks for you. When a client reviews your work, they can immediately tell whether you can be trusted with the task at hand.

Your portfolio should demonstrate:

  • Your niche knowledge such as digital marketing, health, technology, or lifestyle
  • Your writing tone whether formal, conversational, or creative
  • Your structure including headings, flow, transitions, and clarity

You do not need a website yet. Create a Journo portfolio and generate a shareable link to send clients whenever they ask to see your work.

When I began, I created a Journo portfolio. Journo Portfolio allows you to build a professional-looking site to showcase your work using custom pages, collections, and a flexible content builder. You can display your work in a grid, a column, as a blog, or an image gallery. It is free to start and takes less than an hour to set up.

As you grow, consider building a personal website like this one. It gives you complete control over your brand, your positioning, and how clients perceive your work before they even speak to you.

4

Find Your First Clients

Once your samples are ready, it is time to get your first paying gig. Here are the platforms where real opportunities exist:

Upwork

A large marketplace with a high volume of content writing jobs. You create a profile and submit proposals for posted jobs. The platform works for all skill levels, making it a solid starting point. Competition is high but so is the volume of available work.

Fiverr

Lets you list your specific content writing services as Gigs and clients hire you directly. You set your own prices and describe exactly what you offer. Client reviews build your reputation over time and attract higher-paying work.

ProBlogger Job Board

A job board dedicated to blogging and content writing roles. Most postings come from established businesses and blogs that need professional writers. The leads tend to be higher quality than general freelance marketplaces.

LinkedIn

Use the job search function and search for content writer roles. Optimize your profile to attract recruiters and connect with marketing professionals. Many businesses hire writers directly through LinkedIn rather than through freelance platforms.

Contently

Matches established brands with experienced independent writers for content marketing projects. You create a portfolio on their platform and they connect you with opportunities. It requires a strong portfolio but typically offers significantly higher rates than other platforms.

How to Stand Out on Any Platform

Communicate clearly. Deliver on time. Do the work better than the client expected. Those three habits build a freelance career faster than any platform feature or algorithm.

5

Learn How to Price Your Work

My first client paid me $4.17 for a 1,000-word blog post. I was excited until I realized I had spent six hours writing it.

I am still grateful for that experience because my client was satisfied with my work and referred me to more clients. But I learned an important lesson: know your worth before you agree to a rate.

Start at $0.009 per word as a beginner. Raise your rates gradually as your portfolio and confidence grow.

As a beginner, you do not need to charge premium rates immediately. But you do need a baseline so you do not undercharge to the point of working for less than minimum wage. Starting at around $0.009 per word is reasonable for a beginner. A 1,000-word article would earn you approximately $9. As you build your portfolio and develop your niche, raise your rates incrementally. Most experienced content writers charge between $0.05 and $0.20 per word depending on their niche and the complexity of the work.

6

Keep Improving and Building Your Brand

Freelancing is a career you develop over time. It requires patience, consistency, and a willingness to keep learning even when work is slow.

As you write more, make sure you:

  • Continually update your portfolio and LinkedIn bio with your latest and best work
  • Enroll in advanced courses such as SEO certification or copywriting courses from HubSpot
  • Connect with other writers by joining online communities, participating in webinars, and following industry professionals
  • Track your rates and raise them every time you add a significant new skill or credential

After completing my HubSpot certification, I noticed that clients started having greater confidence in my knowledge. That credential gave me an advantage when applying for jobs and allowed me to justify higher rates.

Quick Tips for Beginners

  • Start before you feel ready. You will learn faster by doing than by waiting until conditions are perfect.
  • Write daily. Even one paragraph a day improves your voice and keeps your thinking sharp.
  • Accept feedback without defensiveness. It is the fastest path to improvement.
  • Be reliable above all else. Meeting deadlines consistently builds your reputation faster than talent alone.
  • Stay curious. Trends change fast. The writers who keep learning are the ones who keep earning.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can content writing make me a full-time income?

Yes. Since finishing campus in 2022, I have built a life out of freelance content writing. Consistency and quality are the two non-negotiables.

Can I start freelance content writing with just a phone?

Yes. You can handle simple writing tasks on a phone to get started, then upgrade to a laptop as your income grows and the complexity of your assignments increases.

Can freelance content writing be a full-time career?

Yes. Many writers, including myself, make a full-time income from freelance content writing. It is not a side hustle unless you treat it like one.

What challenges do freelance content writers face?

The most common challenges are inconsistent work volume and clients who pay below market rates. The solution is to diversify your platforms, build direct client relationships, and raise your rates as your portfolio strengthens.

Which content writing niches are most in demand right now?

Health and wellness, technology and SaaS, and finance and investment are currently among the highest-demand niches. These industries need consistent, well-researched content and tend to pay better rates than general lifestyle writing.

Conclusion

If you have interest and intention then those two things are enough to begin.

Freelance content writing offers freedom, continuous learning, and genuine creative satisfaction. The income is real, the career is sustainable, and the barrier to entry is lower than almost any other professional path.

The first step is always the hardest. Start where you are with what you have. Everything else follows from there.

Open HubSpot Academy today. Create your Journo portfolio this week. Send your first proposal before the end of the month. That sequence, done consistently, is how a freelance writing career begins.

The only wrong move is waiting until you feel completely ready. You never will. Start anyway.
